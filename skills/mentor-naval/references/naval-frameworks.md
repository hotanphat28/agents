## Framework 1 — Specific Knowledge Discovery

**The most important career question isn't "What should I do?" It's "What feels like play to me but work to others?"**

### The Discovery Process

**Step 1: The Play Audit**
List activities where you lose track of time, where you'd do it even if no one paid you:
- What did you obsessively do as a child/teenager?
- What do you read/watch/build on weekends voluntarily?
- What conversations make you forget to eat?
- What do people consistently ask you for help with?

**Step 2: The Intersection**
Specific knowledge lives at the intersection of:
- **Genuine curiosity** (you WANT to learn more)
- **Natural ability** (you learn this faster than most)
- **Unique perspective** (your combination of experiences creates an unusual viewpoint)

**Step 3: The Validation Test**
- Can someone be trained to replace you in this area? If yes, it's not specific knowledge.
- Does this feel effortless to you but impressive to others? That's the signal.
- Would you be willing to do this for free for 10 years? If yes, you've found it.

### Coaching Guidance
- "Specific knowledge can't be trained. If society can train someone to do what you do, it will replace you."
- "It's found by pursuing your genuine curiosity, not by following what's hot."
- "Build on it. Don't switch to something 'practical.' The practical thing is to master what only you can do."

## Framework 3 — Wealth vs. Money vs. Status

### The Fundamental Distinction
- **Wealth** = Assets that earn while you sleep. Ownership of businesses, equity, intellectual property, investments.
- **Money** = How we transfer time and wealth. A medium of exchange. A tool.
- **Status** = Your rank in a social hierarchy. Zero-sum game — for you to win, someone must lose.

### The Status Trap
- Status games are addictive: titles, followers, rankings, social comparison
- "Status games will eventually destroy you. Someone always has more."
- "Seek wealth, not status. Wealth creates, status competes."
- "People playing status games will try to attack people playing wealth-creation games."

### Coaching Questions
- "Are you building wealth or chasing status? Be honest."
- "If no one could see your accomplishments, would you still want them?"
- "What would you do if no one was watching?"

## Framework 4 — Happiness Equation

### The Formula
**Happiness = Reality − Expectations**

Or equivalently:
**Happiness = Present − Desire**

### The Practical Implications
1. You can change reality (hard, slow, uncertain)
2. You can change expectations/desires (fast, free, in your control)
3. Most unhappiness comes from the gap between what you have and what you think you should have
4. "Desire is a contract you make with yourself to be unhappy until you get what you want."

### Naval's Happiness Practices
1. **Reduce active desires** — Pick one or two major ones. Release the rest.
2. **Gratitude** — Consciously appreciate what you already have
3. **Meditation** — Practice observing thoughts without attachment
4. **Acceptance** — "In any situation: change it, accept it, or leave it."
5. **Presence** — Happiness is always available in the present moment. It's the future and past that create suffering.

## Framework 5 — The Judgment Framework

**Judgment is the most valuable skill in business and life. It's knowing the long-term consequences of your actions.**

### How to Develop Judgment
1. **Read broadly** — History, psychology, philosophy, biology, physics. Patterns repeat.
2. **Study mental models** — Charlie Munger's approach: collect models from multiple disciplines
3. **Make decisions** — Judgment is developed through practice, not theory
4. **Reflect on outcomes** — After each significant decision, write down what happened and why
5. **Play long-term games** — "Judgment requires patience. Short-term thinking produces bad judgment."

### Key Mental Models (Naval's Favorites)
- **First Principles** — Strip everything to fundamentals and reason from there
- **Compounding** — Small advantages compound into massive ones over time
- **Inversion** — Instead of "how do I succeed?" ask "how would I guarantee failure?" Then avoid that.
- **Second-Order Thinking** — What are the consequences of the consequences?
- **Reversibility** — If the decision is reversible, make it fast. If irreversible, take your time.
- **Optionality** — Prefer decisions that keep options open
- **Skin in the Game** — Don't trust advice from people who don't bear the consequences

## Framework 6 — Principal vs. Agent Mindset

### The Problem
- **Agents** work for someone else's goals, minimizing personal risk, optimizing for salary
- **Principals** own the outcome, take the risk, capture the upside

### The Self-Assessment
| Behavior | Agent | Principal |
|---|---|---|
| Risk | Avoids risk | Takes calculated risks |
| Upside | Capped (salary) | Uncapped (equity) |
| Decision-making | Follows rules | Creates rules |
| Time horizon | Quarter/year | Decade |
| Motivation | External (boss, review) | Internal (mission, ownership) |

### Coaching Questions
- "Are you an agent or a principal in your current situation?"
- "What would it look like to take more ownership of the outcome?"
- "Where can you create equity — even within your current role?"

## Framework 8 — Compounding

### The Three Compounding Domains

**1. Compounding Knowledge**
- "Read what you love until you love to read."
- Start with curiosity, not obligation
- Broad reading > deep specialization (cross-pollination of ideas)
- "The best books are the ones you want to re-read."

**2. Compounding Relationships**
- "Play long-term games with long-term people."
- Invest deeply in a few relationships, not shallowly in many
- Trust is compound interest in human form — takes years to build, seconds to destroy
- "Your network compounds. The relationships you build in your 20s pay dividends in your 40s."

**3. Compounding Wealth**
- Start early, be consistent, be patient
- "The magic of compounding requires long time horizons. Impatience is the enemy."
- Reinvest returns — don't extract, compound
- "The first $100K is the hardest. After that, money starts making money."

### The Compounding Check
"What are you doing today that will be 10x more valuable in 10 years?"
- If you can't name anything → that's the first problem to solve

## Framework 9 — The 40-Hour Experiment

### Finding Specific Knowledge Through Play

**Design:** For one week (40 hours), dedicate your time to the thing you're most curious about. Not what you're "supposed" to do. What you're genuinely drawn to.

**Rules:**
1. No practical justification needed — curiosity is the only criterion
2. No monetization pressure — this is exploration, not a business plan
3. Track energy, not output — what gives you energy? What drains it?
4. Take notes on what surprises you — surprises are signals
5. At the end: "Would I do this again next week for free?"

**If you can't take a full week:**
- 5 hours/week for 8 weeks (same 40 hours, distributed)
- Track the same metrics: energy, curiosity, surprise

**The Signal:** If after 40 hours you want MORE, you've found something worth building on.

## Framework 10 — Decision by Subtraction

### Naval's Decision Framework

**The "Hell Yes" Test:**
- "If you can't decide, the answer is no."
- A clear yes is obvious and energizing. Everything else is a no.
- "When you say yes to something, you're saying no to everything else you could do with that time."

**The Subtraction Method:**
1. List all current commitments, projects, and obligations
2. For each: "If I didn't already have this, would I start it today?"
3. If no → plan to exit or wind down
4. What remains after subtraction is what actually matters

**The Regret Minimization Framework:**
- "Imagine yourself at 80 years old, looking back. Which decision would you regret NOT making?"
- "Regret from action fades. Regret from inaction compounds."

**Naval's Decision Rules:**
- If reversible, decide fast. If irreversible, decide slow.
- If you're torn between two options, choose the harder one (you're probably avoiding it for emotional reasons, not logical ones)
- "In any situation: change it, accept it, or leave it."
